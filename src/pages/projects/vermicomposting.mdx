  <p class="mt-10 mb-10">
    Vermicomposting is the process of using earthworms and their associated microbiomes to break down organic matter into a nutrient-rich soil amendment known as vermicompost or worm castings. Unlike traditional thermophilic composting, which relies on microbial heat generation to decompose organic material, vermicomposting is a cool, continuous process driven by the worm's digestive system and the dense, diverse microbial communities that thrive within it. The result is a biologically active, humus-rich material that improves soil structure, water retention, and nutrient availability.
  </p>

  <p class="mb-10">
    The benefits of vermicompost are well-documented and wide-ranging. Worm castings are rich in plant-available nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, and beneficial microorganisms that support plant health and suppress soil-borne pathogens. Vermicomposting also diverts organic waste from landfills, reducing methane emissions while closing the nutrient loop between food systems and soils. At the CML, <strong>we are particularly interested in the microbial ecology driving these processes</strong> and how the worm gut microbiome shapes the quality and biological activity of the final product.
  </p>

  <p class="mb-10">
    The project aims to reduce campus food waste streams, increase diversion of pre-consumer food waste from dining halls, and raise awareness of sustainable waste management both on and off campus. The living lab serves as a <strong>central outreach hub, offering hands-on workshops and opportunities for NAU students</strong> to develop their own research projects and submit Green Fund proposals.
  </p>

  <p class="mb-10">
    Beyond its role in waste reduction, the living lab functions as an interdisciplinary research and education platform, offering experiential learning opportunities to students across multiple disciplines. Student involvement has supported day-to-day operations, expanded research capacity, and laid the groundwork for future growth and funding opportunities. The living lab also serves as a launchpad for future student-led research initiatives.
  </p>

  <p class="mb-10">
    This project directly <strong>supports NAU's Climate Action Plan</strong> by expanding composting efforts to include vermicomposting, advancing a closed-loop resource management approach to campus sustainability. Beyond emissions reduction, this initiative enhances soil health by producing nutrient-rich vermicompost and worm castings that increase soil organic matter and promote carbon sequestration when applied to NAU's landscapes. By producing high-quality vermicompost, we also reduce reliance on energy-intensive synthetic fertilizers.
  </p>

  <p class="mb-10">
    We also partnered with the SSLUG Garden on campus. Established in 2008, Students for Sustainable Living and Urban Gardening (SSLUG) has long been a hub for hands-on sustainability education at NAU. We conducted a vermicomposting workshop there to teach students and community members how to harness the power of worms to enhance soil quality, support agricultural yields, and honestly, to show everyone just how cool worms really are.
  </p>
